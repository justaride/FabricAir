## Original Requirements

### The Main Question

> "How can Fabric Air access used high quality polyester fibers in Finland to realise its ambitions to develop 100% circular polyester sourcing for its ventilation system?"

### The Six Sub-Questions

1. What sectors/actors use high-quality polyester fibers (durable uniforms, workwear, preferably white)?
2. Who are the main stakeholders in production, use, and reuse?
3. How are polyester textiles disposed of today? What are reuse levels?
4. What regulations govern disposal/reuse? How will they evolve?
5. What public/private initiatives exist? Status, ambition, contacts?
6. **Top 10 most promising sources with contact persons responsible for disposal**

### Key Specifications (from project briefing)

- Material: **100% polyester** (not blends)
- Quality: **High durability** fibers suitable for weaving ventilation ducts
- Color: **White preferred** (for re-dyeing flexibility)
- Typical sources: Industrial workwear, hospital uniforms, road authority clothing

## Critical Gaps Identified

### Gap 1: Material Composition Verification

**Problem:** We assert that cleanroom/pharma garments are "100% polyester, >90% white" but have not:
- Contacted manufacturers to confirm
- Obtained product specifications
- Verified with laundry service providers

**Impact:** FabricAir could approach Orion expecting 100% PET and discover their garments are 99% PET / 1% carbon fiber (ESD protection) â€” which may or may not be compatible.

**Recommendation:** Before commercial outreach, verify material composition directly with Image Wear, Touchpoint, or actual garment manufacturers.

---

### Gap 2: Operational Disposal Contacts

**Problem:** Q6 asked for "persons responsible for disposing of those fibers" â€” we provided strategic/executive contacts instead.

**Impact:** When FabricAir calls hello@rester.fi, they'll reach sales. When they need 500 tonnes of white polyester feedstock, they need the person at HUS who decides where 50,000 worn lab coats go.

**Recommendation:** Phase 2 research should identify:
- Facilities/waste managers at hospitals
- Procurement officers at laundry services
- Sustainability coordinators at food processors

---

### Gap 3: Volume Quantification at Source Level

**Problem:** We know Finland generates 85,770 tonnes of textile waste. We do NOT know:
- How much is workwear vs consumer textiles?
- How much workwear is 100% polyester?
- How much 100% polyester is white?

**Impact:** FabricAir's actual accessible volume could be 500 tonnes or 5,000 tonnes â€” we cannot say.

**Recommendation:** Engage Rester or LSJH to understand actual sorted volumes by composition.

---

### Gap 4: Competitive Intelligence

**Problem:** Meeting notes mention "someone in Finland working on the same thing" â€” potential competition for feedstock. We identified LindstrÃ¶m's Rester investment as a risk but did not:
- Map other companies pursuing polyester feedstock
- Assess Pure Waste, Reima, or other rPET users' supply chains
- Understand how much Rester capacity is already committed

**Impact:** Rester's 6,000-12,000 t/yr capacity may already be spoken for.

**Recommendation:** Before approaching Rester, understand their current customer commitments.

## Honest Assessment: Did We Answer the Main Question?

### The Main Question Again:
> "How can Fabric Air access used high quality polyester fibers in Finland?"

### Our Answer:
We identified **that** access is possible (through Rester, and eventually through NTG/Rewin). We identified **who** the key players are. We identified **what** regulatory changes are coming.

We did **NOT** fully answer **how** FabricAir can access specific volumes of 100% white polyester. The "how" requires:
1. Confirmed availability of compatible material
2. Operational contacts who can supply it
3. Pricing and volume commitments
4. Quality verification

**Verdict:** We provided **market intelligence**, not an **action plan**. The commercial phase plan we created is a template, not a confirmed path.
